\chapter{Presenting the algorithm}

Regev's algorithm can therefore be summarized in the following 12 steps. Let some $d \in \mathbb{Z}$ with $d \approx \sqrt{n}$.\\

1. Create a superposition of all $d$-dimensional vectors $e_1 \dots e_d$, and store them into $d$ quantum registers, weighted by the gaussian function described in Chapter 8.\\
2. Create a quantum register which will contain the superposition $\prod_{i=1}^d a_i^{e_i} \pmod N$, where $a_i$ are the squares of the first $d$ primes, using the procedure described in Chapter 7.\\
3. Measure the product register to obtain some value $u$. This forces the d exponent registers to collapse into a superposition of vectors that satisfy $\prod_{i=1}^d a_i^{e_i} =u$. This represents some coset $\mathit {\Lambda}_t$ of the lattice $\mathit \Lambda$ formed by the vectors that satisfy the above relation for $u = 1$ (Chapter 5).\\
4. Apply QFT to the first register. This turns the superposition in the first d registers into a superposition of dual lattice vectors, and the representative $t$ into a phase applied to the dual lattice vectors.\\
5. Measure the first register and divide the result by $D$ to get a sample of $\Lambda^*$.\\
6. Repeat steps 1 through 5 $d+k$ times to get $d+k$ samples and use them to create a basis of $\Lambda'$.\\
7. Solve SVP on $\mathit \Lambda'$ using LLL, acquiring vectors $\mathbf{e',e'',e''',}\dots$ etc. with coordinates $e'_1 \dots e'_d, e''_1\dots e''_d$ etc. that satisfy the bound from Chapter 9\\
8.For each candidate $\mathbf{e} \in \set{\mathbf{e',e'',e''',\dots}}$ in order, do the following\\
9.Compute $X = \prod_{i=1}^d b_i^{e_{i}} \pmod N$
10. Compute $\gcd(X-1,N)$ to get a factor of N.\\
11. If the factor is trivial, try the next candidate.\\
12. If all factors are trivial, run the algorithm again.

We should also mention that, since the Gaussian splits product-wise, taking the tensor product $d$ times we get a superposition over all d-dimensional vectors.

In terms of space complexity, we will need $O(\sqrt{n})$ quantum registers, each containing values of size up to $2^{n/d} = 2^{\sqrt{n}}$ (so $\sqrt{n}$ qubits), so we have $O(n)$ qubits for the exponent register. The product register contains values up to $N-1$, so we need $O(n)$ qubits for that as well. However, Regev requires approximately $O(\sqrt{n})$ ancillas to compute his modular product, bringing the total qubit count up to $O(n) + O(n)\cdot O(\sqrt{n}) = O(n^{3/2})$. 

Now, the bottleneck for the gates is also modular exponentiation. In our binary tree with $d$ leaves, supposing we precompute up to the second layer, and the only multiplications that are actually performed on the n-bit numbers are the squarings. Notice also that in every binary tree step, in every iteration of the algorithm, the bases are the same, so precomputation saves us a lot of gates. All of this shows that the cost of the binary tree step is negligible compared to the squaring cost. As we described in chapter 7, we perform $O(\sqrt{n})$ squarings, since all exponents are $<2^{O(d)}$, so is the largest exponent, and the number of squarings to be performed is equal to (\# bits in the largest exponent) - 1. Modular squaring (and modular multiplication in general) can be implemented with $O(n \cdot poly(logn))$ gates, in a manner similar to the Schonhage-Strassen algorithm mentioned in the Shor complexity chapter. Summarizing all that, we get $O(n^{3/2} \cdot poly(logn))$ gates.

Lastly, by our results in chapters 6 and 9, setting $k = 4$ gives us a probability that is good enough for all practical purposes. In general, we need $O(d) + $ (some constant number of runs) to have reliable results, so $O(\sqrt{n})$ runs. In short, we have

Qubit complexity: $O(n^{3/2})$ qubits.\\
Gate complexity: $O(n^{3/2} poly(logn))$ gates.\\
Runs: $O(\sqrt{n})$ independent runs with high probability.
